How to Sand Under Cabinets?

Edited byMinhazul Abedin December 2, 2024January 14, 2025

Sanding under cabinets can be a challenging task, often requiring careful planning and the right tools to achieve a smooth and even finish. Whether you are preparing for a new coat of paint, refinishing your cabinets, or addressing wear and tear, knowing how to effectively sand these hard-to-reach areas is crucial.

This guide will provide step-by-step instructions, tips, and techniques to help you successfully sand under cabinets, ensuring a professional-looking result.

Why Sand Under Cabinets?

Importance of Proper Sanding

Proper sanding under cabinets is essential for several reasons:

  • Surface Preparation: Ensures the surface is smooth and ready for painting or staining.
  • Adhesion: Helps new finishes adhere better, leading to a longer-lasting result.
  • Aesthetic Appeal: Removes imperfections, old paint, and finish, resulting in a polished look.

Common Challenges

Sanding under cabinets presents unique challenges, including limited space, awkward angles, and difficulty in maneuvering tools. Overcoming these obstacles requires specific techniques and tools designed for tight spaces.

Tools and Materials Needed

Essential Tools

  1. Detail Sander: Ideal for reaching tight spots and edges.
  2. Sanding Sponges: Flexible and easy to use in confined spaces.
  3. Orbital Sander: Useful for larger, more accessible areas.
  4. Sandpaper Sheets: Various grits (60, 120, 220) for different stages of sanding.
  5. Vacuum Cleaner: For cleaning up dust and debris.
  6. Dust Mask and Safety Goggles: Protects against inhalation of dust and debris.

Optional Tools

  1. Dremel Tool: For intricate areas that are hard to reach with larger tools.
  2. Sanding Blocks: Useful for manual sanding in very tight spots.
  3. Drop Cloths: To protect the surrounding area from dust and debris.

Step-by-Step Guide to Sanding Under Cabinets

Preparation

Clearing the Area

  1. Remove Items: Clear out all items from under the cabinets to ensure an unobstructed work area.
  2. Protect Surroundings: Cover nearby surfaces and floors with drop cloths to protect from dust and debris.

Safety Precautions

  1. Wear Safety Gear: Always wear a dust mask and safety goggles to protect yourself from dust and particles.
  2. Ensure Ventilation: Work in a well-ventilated area to minimize inhalation of dust.

Sanding Process

Choosing the Right Grit

  1. Start with Coarse Grit (60-80): To remove old paint, varnish, or rough surfaces.
  2. Medium Grit (120): For smoothing out the surface after the initial sanding.
  3. Fine Grit (220): To achieve a smooth finish, ready for painting or staining.

Using a Detail Sander

  1. Attach Sandpaper: Attach the appropriate grit sandpaper to the detail sander.
  2. Sand Edges and Corners: Use the detail sander to reach tight spots, edges, and corners under the cabinets.
  3. Maintain Even Pressure: Apply even pressure to avoid gouging the wood.

Sanding with Sanding Sponges

  1. Flexible Tool: Use sanding sponges for areas that are difficult to reach with a detail sander.
  2. Hand Sanding: Sand by hand using the sponge, ensuring you follow the wood grain to prevent scratches.

Using an Orbital Sander

  1. Larger Areas: Use an orbital sander for larger, more accessible areas under the cabinets.
  2. Smooth Finish: Move the sander in a circular motion to achieve an even finish.

Manual Sanding for Tight Spots

  1. Sanding Blocks: Use sanding blocks for very tight spots where power tools cannot reach.
  2. Dremel Tool: For intricate details and hard-to-reach areas, a Dremel tool with a sanding attachment can be very effective.

Cleaning Up

Dust Removal

  1. Vacuum: Use a vacuum cleaner to remove dust and debris from the sanding process.
  2. Wipe Down: Wipe down the sanded surfaces with a damp cloth to remove any remaining dust.

Final Inspection

  1. Smoothness Check: Run your hand over the sanded surfaces to check for smoothness.
  2. Touch-Up: Sand any rough spots that you may have missed.

Tips for Effective Sanding

Working in Stages

  1. Gradual Progression: Start with coarse grit and gradually move to finer grits for the best results.
  2. Patience: Take your time, especially in tight spots, to ensure an even and smooth finish.

Tool Selection

  1. Right Tools: Choose the right tools for the specific area you are sanding. Detail sanders and sanding sponges are essential for tight spaces.
  2. Quality Sandpaper: Invest in high-quality sandpaper to avoid frequent replacements and achieve a better finish.

Safety First

  1. Protective Gear: Always wear safety gear to protect yourself from dust and debris.
  2. Proper Ventilation: Ensure good ventilation to minimize dust inhalation.

Is It Necessary to Sand Cabinets Before Painting?

When considering painting kitchen cabinets, sanding often comes to mind. However, whether sanding is necessary depends on the condition and type of the cabinet. If your cabinets have smooth, non-glossy surfaces, sanding may not be required.

When You Don’t Need to Sand Cabinets

If the cabinets are in good condition, sanding might not be needed. A non-glossy, smooth surface allows paint to adhere well. In such cases, you can clean and prime the cabinets instead.

In many cases, sanding is avoided to save time and effort. For newer cabinets, or cabinets without heavy wear, sanding may be unnecessary. Deglossing with a deglosser or using a bonding primer will suffice.

If your cabinets have a smooth finish without noticeable imperfections, skip sanding. You can skip sanding altogether when using a bonding primer. These primers bond directly to surfaces and create a strong foundation.

When Sanding is Recommended

Sanding is recommended if your cabinets are glossy or damaged. Glossy finishes will prevent paint from sticking properly, leading to peeling. Lightly sanding the surface helps create a better surface for adhesion.

If the cabinets are stained or have rough patches, sand lightly. Use a fine-grit sandpaper to smooth out imperfections before painting. This ensures the paint adheres properly and provides a clean, even finish.

Another reason to sand is if the existing paint is chipped. If the previous finish is in poor condition, sanding removes old layers. After sanding, the surface will be smooth and ready for a fresh coat of paint.

How to Sand Cabinets Properly

When sanding, use fine-grit sandpaper to avoid damaging the wood. Lightly sand the surface in a circular motion to create a smooth finish. Avoid over-sanding, as this can remove the wood’s natural texture.

For best results, use a sanding block or electric sander. A sanding block provides better control over the pressure applied. If using an electric sander, be cautious to avoid uneven sanding.

Once you’ve sanded the cabinets, clean the dust thoroughly before painting. Dust particles can interfere with paint adhesion, leaving a bumpy finish. Use a damp cloth to wipe down the cabinets after sanding.

Benefits of Skipping Sanding

Skipping sanding can save a significant amount of time and effort. It also reduces the mess, as sanding creates dust that can spread. Additionally, it’s easier on your hands, as sanding can be physically demanding.

Using a bonding primer allows you to paint directly without sanding. Bonding primers are specially designed to adhere to slick surfaces. This reduces the number of steps and makes the painting process quicker.

Another advantage of skipping sanding is preserving the existing finish. If the cabinets are in good shape, sanding may weaken the structure. Skipping this step helps maintain the integrity of the wood.

Types of Paint for Cabinets

The type of paint you choose depends on the surface condition. For cabinets that have been sanded, you can use almost any paint. Acrylic latex paints work well for most surfaces and provide excellent coverage.

If you didn’t sand the cabinets, use oil-based paint or bonding primer. Oil-based paints provide a durable finish and are moisture-resistant. Bonding primers help to bond the paint to previously glossy or non-porous surfaces.

For a smooth, factory-like finish, consider using spray paint. Spray paint provides an even coating without brush marks. It’s a good choice if you want a flawless, professional look.

Using a Bonding Primer

A bonding primer is an excellent option when skipping sanding. It creates a grip for the paint, ensuring it sticks to surfaces. When applying a bonding primer, ensure the surface is clean and free from grease.

Bonding primers can be used on various cabinet materials, including wood, laminate, or MDF. These primers often dry quickly, allowing you to paint soon after. Ensure that the primer is completely dry before applying your topcoat.

After priming, follow up with your chosen paint color. Apply at least two thin layers of paint for a smooth finish. Allow each layer to dry completely before applying the next.

Cleaning Cabinets Before Painting

Regardless of whether you sand or not, cleaning is essential. Dirt, grease, and oils can prevent paint from adhering properly. Use a degreaser or a mild cleaner to scrub the surface before painting.

Pay special attention to the areas around handles and hinges. These areas often accumulate grease and food residue over time. After cleaning, rinse the cabinets thoroughly and let them dry completely.

Cleaning not only improves paint adhesion but also enhances the final finish. Dirty cabinets can cause uneven paint application or streaks. A clean surface is the foundation for a smooth, professional paint job.

When to Seek Professional Help

If you’re unsure whether sanding is necessary, a professional can help. They can assess the condition of your cabinets and recommend the best approach. A professional can also handle the proper tools and techniques for sanding and painting.

If your cabinets are heavily damaged or have multiple layers of paint, professional help may be needed. They have the expertise to strip old paint and prepare the cabinets for new finishes. This ensures a high-quality, long-lasting result.

For those unsure about painting techniques or time constraints, professionals offer peace of mind. They can complete the job faster and with more precision. If you want the best results with minimal hassle, a pro might be the best option.

Final Words

Sanding under cabinets can be a daunting task, but with the right tools, techniques, and patience, you can achieve a smooth and professional finish. Proper preparation, careful selection of tools, and following a step-by-step process are key to overcoming the challenges posed by tight spaces and awkward angles.

By adhering to the guidelines provided in this article, you can ensure that your sanding project under cabinets is successful, resulting in a beautifully finished surface ready for painting or staining.
